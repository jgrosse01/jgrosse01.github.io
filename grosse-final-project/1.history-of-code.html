History of Code

The First Computer

The first contraption which resembled a computer was invented in 1801 by Joseph Marie Jacquard. This loom utilized wooden cards similar to the modern concept of a punch card to automatically weave more complex fabric designs.

True computers came slightly later in the mid 1940s. The computer was created by John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert, two professors from University of Pennsylvania. The computer was called the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Calculator (ENIAC) and filled a 20 by 40 foot room! It contained 18,000 vacuum tubes.

In the 50s, the first high-functioning programming language was released to communicate with computers. It was created by John Mauchly, one creator of the ENIAC, and was called "Short Code." Languages like this led to the later development of languages like A and B, and shortly after the release of B, the first modern programming language, C, was released in 1972.

Modern computers were based off the ideas of Alan Turing, the father of Artificial Intelligence, who theorized that there would one day be a computer which could compute everything which was computable. It is Turing's vision which sparked the movement to build Artificial Intelligences and the creation of deep learning algorithms.

In the year 1983, Apple introduced Graphical User Interfaces (GUIs) to computing with Lisa, the predecessor to the modern Macintosh. These GUIs led to the later development of PCs as gaming devices with the release of games like "Command & Conquer," "Alone in the Dark 2," "Magic Carpet," and "Descent" hitting the markets.

Today, languages like Java, Python, Unreal Script, and C++ are used to create the modern gaming titles that we know such as "The Elder Scrolls" series, "Wolfenstein," "The Witcher," "Assassin's Creed," and "Minecraft."
